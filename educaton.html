CASTER

As medical research advances, so does the demand for ways to discover and analyze new data. One area of biomedical research is to identify specific genes and the effects they have in the body. Once genes are better understood, scientists can discover better treatment options. A big holdup on cellular analysis is how long it takes to identify structures and to see how they interact with whatâ€™s around them. CASTER is a program designed to speed up this process by automatically outlining membranes. This program uses contrast within the image and a line of best fit. From there, the user can display a 3D image to help analyze the data.

To get the image stack, a process known as Serial Block-Face Imaging was used. Mice were used to obtain the biological samples.

Serial Block-Face Imaging

3View serial block face imaging from Gatan Inc. on Vimeo.

Step-by-Step Workflow

  1. Sample preparation: Fix, stain with a contrasting agent and embed in resin for stability.
  2. Mount and transfer to SEM: Trim the sample and mounted to an aluminum pin. A thin gold sputter coating is optional. Transfer to the 3View and bring the diamond knife into contact with the sample.
  3. Set imaging parameters: Select beam conditions, magnification, pixel count and dwell time. This sets your resolution, field-of-view and acquisition time.
  4. Automated imaging: Sequential images are acquired. The knife automatically removes a layer before each image.
  5. Analysis: The images are stacked to construct a 3D dataset. This can be processed and viewed in Gatanâ€™s DigitalMicrograph or third-party software. Segmentation and quantification can be performed.

Warwick, T. (2018). What is Serial Block-Face Imaging? (SBEM) | Blue Scientific. [online] Blue Scientific.

Results

The first way to use CASTER is to set the number of boxes made to zero, turning off the automatic membrane outlining. This is helpful in areas where the contrast is not well defined. As the user drags their mouse, the program selects the points in that area that are most likely part of the membrane.

The second way is to set the number of boxes to anything else, and the program will highlight the well-defined membrane. The selected points are based on how the sliders were set. The user clicks on a single point on the membrane, and the program works outwards from there to select the membrane.
